WHAT IS IT?
-----------
Identity is the system that decides who is trusted to do what in a cloud environment.
It is not a feature. It is not a setting. It is a control plane.

Infrastructure determines what can exist.
Identity determines who is trusted to act on it.

Week 1 is about building the mental model before touching any tools.
You will not write a script this week. You will not touch a console.
You will learn to think about identity the way a real engineer does.

---

KEY SERVICES & TERMS
---------------------
Identity
    The system that answers: "Who are you and should I trust you?"
    In real systems, identity controls access to people, data, and infrastructure.

Authentication
    Proving who you are.
    Example: Entering your password or presenting a certificate.
    Authentication answers: "Are you who you claim to be?"

Authorization
    Proving what you are allowed to do.
    Example: Your role allows read access but not write access.
    Authorization answers: "Are you allowed to do this?"

Trust
    The relationship between systems that allows one to accept identity claims from another.
    Example: GCP trusts Microsoft Entra ID to authenticate users.
    Trust is not automatic. It is configured, deliberate, and fragile.

Principal
    Who the policy applies to. Can be a user, a service account, or a group.

Least Privilege
    Only grant the minimum access required to do the job. Nothing more.

Blast Radius
    How much damage can be done if an identity is compromised or misconfigured.
    Small blast radius = mistake affects one resource.
    Large blast radius = mistake affects an entire organization.

Lockout
    When a valid user or system loses access due to an identity failure.
    A lockout is an outage. It stops work just like a server going down.

Audit Trail / Log
    The record of who did what and when.
    Program mantra: "If it's not logged, it didn't happen."

---

HOW IT WORKS
------------
In a cloud system, identity works in layers:

1. A user or service tries to access a resource.
2. The identity provider (e.g., Microsoft Entra ID) authenticates the user.
   -- It checks: are you who you say you are?
3. The cloud platform (e.g., GCP) checks authorization.
   -- It checks: are you allowed to do this action on this resource?
4. If both checks pass, access is granted.
5. Every step is logged.

The critical point:
    Authentication and authorization are separate.
    Passing authentication does not guarantee authorization.
    You can prove who you are and still be denied.

Trust is the glue between these layers.
If trust breaks (e.g., a certificate expires, a federation config changes),
the entire chain breaks — even if the user's credentials are valid.

---

THINGS TO REMEMBER
------------------
1. Identity failures cause outages.
   A server going down is visible and recoverable.
   An identity failure can silently lock out an entire team, and recovery is harder.

2. Access recovery is harder than deployment rollback.
   If you deploy bad code, you roll it back.
   If you misconfigure identity, you may lock out the very account
   needed to fix the mistake. This is why identity changes are slow and deliberate.

3. Authentication is not authorization.
   Logging in successfully does not mean you can do what you need to do.
   Both layers must work.

4. Trust is fragile.
   Trust relationships between systems (federation) can break due to:
   -- Certificate expiration
   -- Configuration changes
   -- Policy drift
   When trust breaks, everyone who depends on it loses access simultaneously.

5. Blast radius always matters.
   Before any identity change, ask:
   "If this goes wrong, what is the maximum damage?"
   Start small. Test in isolation. Never change production identity config without a recovery plan.

6. Logs are evidence.
   Gut feelings and screen grabs are not evidence.
   Logs are. If you cannot find it in a log, you cannot prove it happened.

7. AI is not the decision maker.
   AI can help you analyze and summarize.
   AI cannot and should not make identity decisions.
   You are accountable for every change you make.

REAL WORLD ANALOGY
------------------
Think of a large hospital.

Infrastructure = the building, the rooms, the equipment.
Identity = the badge system that controls who can enter which room.

Authentication = your badge identifies you as Dr. Smith.
Authorization = your badge allows you into the operating rooms but not the pharmacy.
Trust = the hospital trusts badges issued by its own HR system.

A lockout is when Dr. Smith's badge stops working at 2am during surgery prep.
The infrastructure (the OR) still exists. The equipment still works.
But the surgeon cannot get in. That is an outage.

Access recovery = getting Dr. Smith back in without compromising the pharmacy.
This takes time, deliberate steps, and a chain of trusted people.
You cannot just override the badge system blindly.

---

USE CASES
---------
Identity control applies everywhere:

- A cloud engineer needs access to a GCP project to deploy infrastructure.
  Identity determines whether they can.

- An automated pipeline (CI/CD) needs to write to a storage bucket.
  A service account identity controls whether it can.

- A company merges with another company and needs to share systems.
  Federation extends trust so users from Company B can access Company A's GCP resources
  without creating new accounts.

- An engineer leaves the company.
  Revoking their identity immediately removes all access across all systems.
  This is why identity is the control plane, not the firewall.
