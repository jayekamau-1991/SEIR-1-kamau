SECTION 1: THE BIG PICTURE
----------------------------

Core Statement (Memorize This)
    Infrastructure decides what can exist.
    Identity decides who is trusted.

What This Means In Practice
    You can build the most secure server in the world.
    If identity is misconfigured, the wrong person gets in.
    Or the right person gets locked out.
    Either way, the infrastructure is irrelevant.

    Can you explain in one sentence what identity controls?
    Can you explain why identity is called the "control plane"?

SECTION 2: THE THREE CONCEPTS
-------------------------------

Authentication
    What it is: Proving who you are.
    The question it answers: Are you who you claim to be?
    Example: You enter your password. The system confirms your identity.
    Key point: Authentication alone does not give you access.

Authorization
    What it is: Proving what you are allowed to do.
    The question it answers: Are you permitted to do this action?
    Example: You are authenticated as a developer.
             But your role only allows read access. Not write.
    Key point: Authorization happens AFTER authentication.

Trust
    What it is: A configured relationship where one system accepts
                identity claims from another.
    The question it answers: Do I accept what this other system tells me about this user?
    Example: GCP trusts Microsoft Entra ID to verify your identity.
    Key point: Trust is not automatic. It is deliberate, configured, and fragile.

Check Yourself
    What is the difference between authentication and authorization?
    Can you be authenticated but not authorized? Give an example.
    What happens when trust breaks between two systems?

SECTION 3: WHY IDENTITY FAILURES ARE WORSE
--------------------------------------------

Scenario A — Infrastructure Failure
    A server goes down.
    Engineers see an alert.
    They restart the server or roll back the deployment.
    Recovery time: minutes to hours.
    Evidence: logs, metrics, alerts.

Scenario B — Identity Failure
    An admin misconfigures a permission group.
    Users start losing access silently.
    No server is down. No obvious alert fires.
    The person who needs to fix it may be locked out.
    Recovery requires finding a trusted account that still has access.
    Recovery time: hours to days.
    Evidence: audit logs — if they were enabled.

Why Identity Is Harder To Recover
    Infrastructure rollback: redeploy the last known good version.
    Identity rollback: you may have locked out the account needed to perform the rollback.
    This is why identity changes must be slow, deliberate, and always have a recovery plan.

Check Yourself
    Why is a lockout considered an outage?
    Why is access recovery harder than deployment rollback?
    What would you check first if users suddenly lost access to a GCP project?

SECTION 4: BLAST RADIUS
------------------------

Definition
    Blast radius = how much damage can occur if an identity change goes wrong.

Small Blast Radius Example
    You change permissions for one service account used by one pipeline.
    If it breaks, one pipeline fails. Nothing else is affected.

Large Blast Radius Example
    You change permissions for an admin group.
    Every user in that group is affected.
    Every system those users manage is potentially affected.
    One mistake. Organization-wide impact.

The Rule
    Before any identity change, ask:
    "If this goes wrong, what is the maximum damage?"
    If the answer is large — slow down, test in isolation, have a recovery plan.

Check Yourself
    A teammate wants to modify the permissions on the primary admin group
    to fix a bug. What questions do you ask before they proceed?
    What does least privilege have to do with blast radius?

SECTION 5: LOGS AND EVIDENCE
------------------------------

Program Mantra
    "If it's not logged, it didn't happen."

What This Means
    In identity systems, feelings are not evidence.
    Screenshots of a working screen are not evidence.
    Logs are evidence.

What Logs Tell You
    Who accessed what resource
    When the access happened
    Whether access was granted or denied
    What changed and who changed it

Why This Matters For Identity
    If a permissions change breaks something, the log tells you:
    -- What changed
    -- Who made the change
    -- When it happened
    Without logs, you are guessing. Guessing in identity systems is dangerous.

Check Yourself
    A junior engineer says "I fixed it, my screen looks fine."
    What three things are wrong with that statement?
    Where would you look to prove an identity change was made in GCP?
