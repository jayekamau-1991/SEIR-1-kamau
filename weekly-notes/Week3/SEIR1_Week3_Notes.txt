WHAT IS IT?
-----------
Week 3 moves from mental models into the actual structure of Google Cloud Platform.

Before you can manage identity in GCP you need to understand how GCP organizes
resources and who is allowed to act on them. That structure is built around
three things: organizations, folders, and projects.

Identity in GCP is enforced through IAM — Identity and Access Management.
IAM is not the same as Entra ID. They serve different purposes.
This week you learn the difference and why it matters.

---

KEY SERVICES & TERMS
---------------------

GCP Resource Hierarchy
    GCP organizes everything in a hierarchy from top to bottom:
        Organization → Folders → Projects → Resources

    Organization
        The top level. Represents your entire company in GCP.
        All projects belong to the organization.
        Policies set at this level affect everything below it.

    Folders
        Optional grouping layer between organization and projects.
        Used to organize projects by team, department, or environment.
        Example: A folder for Production, a folder for Development.
        Policies set on a folder affect all projects inside it.

    Projects
        The primary unit of work in GCP.
        Every resource (VM, storage bucket, database) lives inside a project.
        Billing, APIs, and IAM permissions are managed at the project level.
        Projects are blast radius boundaries.

    Resources
        The actual things you build — VMs, buckets, databases, networks.
        Resources live inside projects.

GCP IAM (Identity and Access Management)
    The system that controls who can do what inside GCP.
    IAM works by attaching roles to principals on specific resources.
    GCP IAM is NOT the same as Entra ID.
    GCP IAM = what you can do inside GCP.
    Entra ID = who you are and whether you are trusted.

Principal
    Who the IAM policy applies to.
    Can be:
        - A Google account (human user)
        - A service account (non-human, automated)
        - A Google group
        - A federated identity from Entra ID

Role
    A collection of permissions bundled together.
    Instead of assigning individual permissions, you assign roles.
    Three types:
        Basic roles — Owner, Editor, Viewer (broad, avoid in production)
        Predefined roles — specific to a service (e.g. Storage Admin)
        Custom roles — you define exactly what permissions are included

Binding
    The connection between a principal and a role on a resource.
    Example: "User A has the role Storage Viewer on Project B"
    That is a binding.

Service Account
    A special type of identity used by machines, not humans.
    Used by applications, VMs, and automation tools like Terraform.
    Service accounts are NOT for humans to log in with.
    They authenticate using keys or workload identity, not passwords.

Policy Inheritance
    IAM policies set at a higher level in the hierarchy
    are inherited by everything below it.
    Organization policy → inherited by all folders and projects
    Folder policy → inherited by all projects in that folder
    Project policy → inherited by all resources in that project
    This is powerful and dangerous. A policy at the org level
    affects everything in the organization.

Least Privilege
    Grant only the minimum permissions required to do the job.
    Nothing more. This limits blast radius when something goes wrong.

Blast Radius Boundary
    Projects are the primary blast radius boundary in GCP.
    If a permission is misconfigured in Project A,
    it cannot affect Project B unless policies are set at a higher level.
    This is why you separate environments into separate projects.

---

HOW IT WORKS
------------
When a principal tries to perform an action in GCP:

1. GCP identifies the principal.
   Who is making this request? A human, a service account, a federated identity?

2. GCP checks the IAM policy for the resource being accessed.
   Is there a binding that grants this principal permission to do this action?

3. GCP checks policy inheritance.
   If no binding exists at the resource level, it checks the project level.
   Then the folder level. Then the organization level.

4. If a matching binding is found — action is permitted.
   If no matching binding is found — action is denied.

5. Everything is logged in Cloud Audit Logs.

The key point:
    Deny by default. If there is no explicit Allow, the action is blocked.
    You must explicitly grant access. Access is never assumed.

---

THINGS TO REMEMBER
------------------
1. Projects are blast radius boundaries.
   Keep Production, Staging, and Development in separate projects.
   A mistake in Development cannot reach Production if they are isolated.

2. Service accounts are not for humans.
   Never give a human a service account to log in with.
   Service accounts are for automated systems and applications only.
   Humans authenticate through Entra ID or their Google account.

3. Avoid basic roles in production.
   Owner, Editor, and Viewer are too broad.
   Owner on a project means you can do anything — delete VMs, change billing, modify IAM.
   Use predefined or custom roles with least privilege instead.

4. Policy inheritance flows downward.
   A role granted at the organization level applies to every project.
   Be extremely careful what you assign at the top of the hierarchy.
   Blast radius at the org level = entire company.

5. GCP IAM and Entra ID are not the same.
   Entra ID handles authentication — who you are.
   GCP IAM handles authorization — what you can do.
   Both must work correctly for access to succeed.

6. Always check blast radius before adding or changing a role.
   Ask: "If I grant this role here, what else does it affect?"
   A role granted at the folder level affects every project in that folder.

7. Logs are your evidence.
   Cloud Audit Logs record every IAM change, every access attempt,
   every permission grant and denial.
   If you cannot find it in the logs, you cannot prove it happened.

REAL WORLD ANALOGY
------------------
Think of GCP's resource hierarchy like a company building.

Organization = the entire company building
Folders = floors of the building (Finance floor, Engineering floor)
Projects = individual offices on each floor
Resources = the desks, computers, and files inside each office

IAM is the badge access system.

A badge granted at the building level gets you into every floor and every office.
A badge granted at the floor level gets you into every office on that floor.
A badge granted at the office level gets you into only that office.

A service account is like a robot that works in a specific office.
It has a badge that only works for the tasks it needs to do.
You would never give a human employee a robot badge to log in with.
And you would never give the robot a master key to the whole building.

Least privilege = give everyone only the badge they need for their job.
Nothing more.

USE CASES
---------
- A DevOps engineer needs to deploy VMs in the Production project.
  They are granted the Compute Instance Admin role on that project only.
  They cannot touch the Finance project or the Development project.
  Blast radius is contained to one project.

- Terraform needs to create infrastructure in GCP.
  A service account called terraform-service is created.
  It is granted only the roles it needs — Storage Admin, Compute Admin.
  Terraform authenticates as the service account using a JSON key.
  No human credentials are stored anywhere.

- A new team joins the organization.
  A folder is created for their projects.
  IAM policies are set at the folder level for that team.
  All projects they create inside that folder inherit the policies automatically.

- An engineer accidentally grants Owner to the wrong user at the project level.
  Because it is at the project level, only that project is affected.
  Other projects are not impacted.
  The mistake is contained by the blast radius boundary.
